Scintillation detector

The detector we used for our measurements is a scintillation detector which harnesses interactions between cosmic rays, subatomic particles coming from space, and scintillator material. Because these particles are charged, they cause electrons of the material to shift to the outer shell, so the atom turns from the fundamental to the excited state.
When electrons come back to the fundamental state, photons are emitted. This radiation is gathered and transmitted through a light guide to a photomultiplier, which generates electric current; this current is finally analysed by an electronic system.

• Our scintillator consists in two transparent plastic slabs which have these characteristics:
          o Refractive index: 1.58
          o Wavelength: 425 nm
          o Max solid angle: 2.6 sr
It is important that particles hit both slabs to determine a coincidence measurement; in this way we avoid interference of Earth’s radiation and data are more trustworthy.

• The light guide is a plexiglass component of the detector which has refractive index of 1.58 and a total reflection.
Its function is to convey light into the photomultiplier thanks to a series of reflections.

• The photomultiplier consists in: a photocathode, which emits electrons if hit by radiation (according to photoelectric effect), dynodes, which multiply electrons in a waterfall effect, and the anode, which is the point where the beam of electrons is conveyed, ready to be measured.
